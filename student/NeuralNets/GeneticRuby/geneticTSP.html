  1. Introduction
    As described elsewhere the Travelling Salesman Problem is a problem that can't be solved in polynomial time. This paper describes a genetic algorithm to find approximate solutions to the problem. Genetic solutions are solutions that follow an algorithm simillar to that proposed for the genetic transformation in the evolution of species. The idea is to combine "good" solutions to find more, hopefully better solutions. In the evolutionary example the hope is that "good" solutions are the more common ones. If they are more common, they are more likely to combine or breed and produce more good solutions and some better ones as well.

    The travelling salesman problem is suited to this algorithm particularly well because its easy to define "good", and its easy to take two solutions and recombine them into a better solution. A good solution, obviously, is a short one. Recombining two solutions is a little more complicated than just slicing them and putting them back together, but its close. You need to make sure each city is in the resulting tour.

    Another whole twist is that there may be better solutions than those present in the population. There may be a solution that is better than the best combination of all the parts in the population. A mutation is a random change that may or may not exist in any population member. This algorithm uses two different mutation operations to randomly introduce new combinations into the population. Details are in the assigment.

    This particular implementation is written in Ruby, a newer scripting language simillar to Perl and Python. I was amazed at how easy and familliar it is to use. It was much easier than when I was learning Perl. I think Ruby is easier than Perl because the syntax uses less cryptic characters. It might just be that Ruby was easy for me since I had already struggled with Perl. The Gui is written using Tk, a toolkit used with Perl or another scripting language Tcl.

    Observations
    I noticed, as suggested in the assignment, that without mutations, the system finds a good solution rather quickly. It levels out almost immediately. My graphs only go up to 500 generations,  since the code was written in Ruby. It runs slow. 500 generations took about 5 minutes. Without mutations, the bottom of the graph, the shortest tour,  was found rather quickly. With mutations, both kinds, the bottom slowly drifts lower. While the tops of the mutated runs are erratic and higher than the non mutated one, it seems the mutations help a little bit. I need to let a mutated run go overnight and see how well the slow improvement does.
